▾ Chapter 1 — The CIA Triad and threat fundamentals

(Source: Day 01 — Cybersecurity Fundamentals, Lecture 1, §§ 1.1 – 1.3)

Every security control on Earth ultimately props up one of three letters: C, I, or A. Lose any one of them and you have a breach, regardless of how clever the other two were.

Confidentiality is the promise that only authorised eyes ever see the data. The textbook example in the source material is a Montréal dental clinic whose receptionist account had been handed Full Control of the entire \\CLINIC-SRV\Patients share — "because it was easier than fixing it later." When a stolen laptop carrying those credentials ended up on a flea market, fourteen thousand patient X-rays became browsable by whoever bought the machine. That is a textbook Confidentiality failure caused by a textbook violation of the principle of least privilege — give every user, process, and service the minimum permissions needed to do its job, and not one click more. The principle is so central that NIST — National Institute of Standards and Technology elevates it from advice to a formal control under SP 800-53 AC-6.

Mechanisms that protect Confidentiality: encryption at rest, encryption in transit, Access Control Lists (ACLs — Access Control Lists), Role-Based Access Control (RBAC), Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA), and (above all) least privilege.

Integrity is the promise that the data you read is the data that was written, with no unauthorised change in between. A €10 transfer that becomes €10,000 mid-flight, a log file silently edited by an attacker to erase footprints, an operating-system update silently swapped for a malicious one — all three are Integrity failures, on three completely different time-scales. The classroom example: a Cisco router configuration in a small co-working space, whose Sunday-night backup hash differs from the previous week's stored baseline. Investigation reveals a junior tech who quietly added a permit any any rule "to test something." That single byte change was caught only because the hash changed — the avalanche effect of SHA-256 (Secure Hash Algorithm 256-bit) means even a one-byte edit produces a wholly different fingerprint.

Tools that protect Integrity: cryptographic hashes (SHA-256, SHA-3), digital signatures, code-signing certificates, File Integrity Monitoring (FIM) tools such as AIDE (Advanced Intrusion Detection Environment) or OSSEC, and write-once-read-many (WORM) logging.

Availability is the promise that the system and the data are reachable when authorised users actually need them. A perfectly encrypted database that nobody can connect to is a security failure. A hospital whose patient-record system has been ransomware-encrypted during a heart attack is a security failure on the A axis specifically. So is a Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS — Distributed Denial of Service) attack against a bank on payday. Defences: redundancy via Redundant Array of Independent Disks (RAID), failover clusters, geographic load-balancing, DDoS scrubbing services such as Cloudflare or AWS Shield, regularly tested backups, and capacity planning.

A reference table from the source material that you will probably see again on the exam:

Tier Target uptime Allowed downtime per year Allowed downtime per month
Basic 99 % 3 days 15 hours 7 h 18 min
Standard 99.9 % ("three nines") 8 h 45 min 43 min
High 99.99 % ("four nines") 52 min 4.3 min
Critical 99.999 % ("five nines") 5.3 min 26 seconds

A reminder that nobody on the textbook side of the field likes to say out loud: a perfectly secure system is unplugged, encased in concrete, and dropped into the Mariana Trench — and is therefore useless. Every security control has a cost: encryption uses CPU (Central Processing Unit), logging uses disk, deep packet inspection adds latency. The job of a Security Analyst is not to maximise security but to balance it against the business's need to actually function. That tension is element 1.4 of competency EZ01 and it will come up under several disguises on the exam.

▾ Chapter 2 — The cyber-threat bestiary

(Source: Day 01 — Cybersecurity Fundamentals, Lecture 1, § 1.4)

You cannot defend against what you cannot name. The taxonomy below is the one to memorise.

Malware family tree. Each entry has a behaviour, not just a name — the behaviour is what an exam will test.

Category Defining behaviour Real-world example
Virus Infects a host file; runs when the host runs ILOVEYOU (2000)
Worm Self-propagates across networks, no user action WannaCry (2017)
Trojan Disguises itself as legitimate software Emotet
Ransomware Encrypts files, demands payment LockBit, Conti
Spyware / Stalkerware Silently exfiltrates user activity Pegasus
Rootkit Hides at OS (Operating System) or firmware level Stuxnet's rootkit component
Cryptojacker Mines cryptocurrency on the victim's CPU/GPU Coinhive (in-browser)
Remote Access Trojan (RAT) Gives the attacker full remote control of a host DarkComet, NjRAT

Network-level attacks. Denial of Service (DoS) and Distributed DoS (DDoS) flood a service until legitimate users can no longer reach it. Man-In-The-Middle (MITM) sits between two endpoints to intercept or modify traffic. ARP (Address Resolution Protocol) spoofing abuses the trusting nature of ARP to redirect traffic on a Local Area Network (LAN). Domain Name System (DNS) poisoning or hijacking corrupts DNS responses to redirect victims to malicious sites. Port scanning and enumeration is the reconnaissance step that precedes almost every other attack — the reason your firewall logs are full of probes that never quite turn into intrusions.

Human-targeted attacks (social engineering). Phishing is mass-deceptive email aiming for credentials or payment. Spear phishing is its targeted, personalised cousin. Whaling targets executives specifically. Vishing (voice-phishing) and smishing (SMS-phishing) are the phone and text-message variants. Business Email Compromise (BEC) impersonates an executive to authorise a wire transfer — by dollar value it is the single most damaging attack class year after year, according to the FBI Internet Crime Complaint Center (IC3) annual reports.

Application-layer attacks. SQL Injection (SQLi — Structured Query Language Injection) injects database commands via unfiltered input. Cross-Site Scripting (XSS) injects scripts that run in another user's browser. Cross-Site Request Forgery (CSRF) tricks a logged-in user's browser into submitting unwanted actions. Server-Side Request Forgery (SSRF) tricks a server into making attacker-chosen requests, often deep into internal networks the attacker could not reach directly. File-upload vulnerabilities are the classic: a misconfigured server happily executes .php or .aspx files dropped by an unauthenticated visitor.

Supply-chain and insider attacks. A supply-chain compromise infects a vendor's update mechanism — SolarWinds in 2020 and the xz-utils backdoor CVE-2024-3094 are the canonical recent cases. Insider threats are the legitimate users (malicious or merely negligent) with whom no perimeter defence can help you. A zero-day exploit abuses a vulnerability before a patch exists; the defender's only option is detection-in-depth.

A mapping table that appears verbatim in the source material — memorise it:

Threat Primary CIA leg attacked Typical defence
Ransomware A (and C if data is exfiltrated first) Tested offline backups, EDR (Endpoint Detection and Response)
DDoS A Anycast, scrubbing centres, rate-limiting
SQLi C and I Parameterised queries, Web Application Firewall (WAF)
Phishing C User training, MFA, email gateway
MITM C and I TLS (Transport Layer Security), certificate pinning
Insider data theft C Data Loss Prevention (DLP), least privilege, User and Entity Behaviour Analytics (UEBA)
Supply-chain C, I, A Software Bill of Materials (SBOM), code-signing, vendor risk
▾ Chapter 3 — Cryptography primitives — and when to use each

(Source: Day 01 — Cybersecurity Fundamentals, Lecture 2, §§ 2.1 – 2.2)

There are three primitive families, and confusing them is how vulnerabilities get written into production code.

Symmetric encryption. Same secret key for both encrypt and decrypt. Fast — measured in gigabytes per second — but you need a secure channel to share the key first. That bootstrap problem is solved by asymmetric crypto (next).

Algorithm Status (2025) Typical use
AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) — 128, 256 ✅ Recommended Disk encryption (BitLocker, LUKS), TLS body, S3 server-side encryption
ChaCha20 ✅ Recommended Mobile TLS, WireGuard VPN
3DES (Triple Data Encryption Standard) ⚠️ Deprecated Legacy banking only
DES, RC4 ❌ Broken Never use

Asymmetric encryption. A key pair — public + private. Anything encrypted with one can only be decrypted by the other. Slow (measured in kilobytes per second), so it is used for key exchange and digital signatures, not bulk data.

Algorithm Status Typical use
RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman) — 2048, 3072 ✅ Acceptable TLS certificates, PGP — Pretty Good Privacy
ECDSA (Elliptic Curve Digital Signature Algorithm), Ed25519 ✅ Recommended TLS, SSH keys, code signing
RSA-1024 ❌ Broken Never
Post-quantum (ML-KEM, ML-DSA) 🟡 Standardised 2024 Future-proofing

Hash functions. One-way. Take any input, produce a fixed-length fingerprint. Cannot be reversed. Used for integrity verification, password storage (with salt), and digital signatures.

Function Status Notes
SHA-256, SHA-3-256 ✅ Recommended Default for everything
BLAKE2, BLAKE3 ✅ Recommended Faster than SHA-2
SHA-1 ⚠️ Broken for collisions Avoid; still appears in Git for the moment
MD5 (Message Digest 5) ❌ Fully broken Never for security

The combined pattern — asymmetric to bootstrap, symmetric for bulk, hash for integrity — is in fact what every TLS 1.3 handshake performs in a few milliseconds, every time you load an HTTPS (HyperText Transfer Protocol Secure) page. Internalise that one sentence and you will pass every cryptography question on the exam.

A specific gotcha that is almost certainly going to be tested: never hash passwords with plain SHA-256. The right answer is a deliberately slow Key Derivation Function (KDF) with a per-user random salt — historically bcrypt, since 2021 the OWASP (Open Web Application Security Project) top recommendation is Argon2id. The reason: SHA-256 is fast, an attacker tries ten billion guesses per second on a Graphics Processing Unit (GPU), and without a salt identical passwords produce identical hashes, leaking information across users.

▾ Chapter 4 — Security frameworks and risk

(Source: Day 01 — Cybersecurity Fundamentals, Lecture 2, §§ 2.3 – 2.4)

You will not invent your security programme from scratch. Three institutions have already done the work.

NIST — National Institute of Standards and Technology (USA). A US federal agency that publishes free, world-class cybersecurity standards. The four documents you must recognise on sight:
- NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0 — six functions: Govern, Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover. This is the lingua franca of risk conversations between technical staff and executives.
- NIST SP 800-53 Revision 5 — a catalogue of more than a thousand security controls organised into twenty families (AC = Access Control, AU = Audit, IR = Incident Response, and so on). Mandatory for US federal systems; voluntary but widely adopted elsewhere.
- NIST SP 800-61 Revision 2 — the gold-standard incident-response playbook.
- NIST National Vulnerability Database (NVD) — every Common Vulnerabilities and Exposures (CVE) entry in one searchable place.

ISO — International Organization for Standardization and IEC — International Electrotechnical Commission. Together they publish the 27000-series. The two documents that matter for the exam:
- ISO/IEC 27001:2022 — the certifiable Information Security Management System (ISMS) standard. When a company says "we are ISO 27001 certified," it means an external auditor has confirmed they manage security as a continuous process.
- ISO/IEC 27002:2022 — the catalogue of ninety-three controls referenced by 27001.

CIS — Center for Internet Security. A US non-profit. Two products you will use:
- CIS Critical Security Controls v8.1 — a prioritised list of eighteen controls. If you have nothing in place yet, this is where you start.
- CIS Benchmarks — line-by-line hardening guides for Windows, Linux, AWS, Azure, Cisco, and more than a hundred other platforms.

A side note on threat-intel sources you will be expected to know by name:
- CISA — Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency (USA) — US federal cyber-defence agency. Publishes free advisories that name names.
- Canadian Centre for Cyber Security (CCCS) — Canada's federal equivalent. Read its quarterly threat assessments.
- MITRE ATT&CK — a catalogue of adversary tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). Every modern Security Information and Event Management (SIEM) tool ships rules tagged with ATT&CK technique IDs.
- MITRE CVE — the canonical CVE register.
- AlienVault OTX (Open Threat Exchange) — community threat-intel feed.
- Shodan — search engine for Internet-exposed services. Read-only browsing only; no scanning from the lab.
- Have I Been Pwned — Troy Hunt's free credential-leak lookup.

Risk assessment. The formula is Risk = Likelihood × Impact. Estimate likelihood from threat intelligence and historical incidents; estimate impact from business value at stake. Then pick controls until residual risk drops below the business's risk appetite. A worked example from the source material:

Asset Threat Likelihood (1–5) Impact (1–5) Risk score Mitigation control
Patient database Ransomware 4 5 20 NIST PR.DS-1 (offline backups)
Public website DDoS 3 3 9 Cloudflare WAF + autoscaling
Employee laptops Phishing 5 3 15 MFA + email gateway + training

A subtle point that often catches students: the score is just a number. The decision the business actually makes is whether to mitigate, transfer, accept, or avoid each risk — that decision is owned by the business, not by you as the analyst. Your job is to make the trade-offs visible and accurate.

▾ Chapter 5 — Network types and topology shapes

(Source: Days 03–04 — Network Topology, Lecture 1, Sections 1–2)

Before you draw a single line between a router and a switch, you must answer one question: what kind of network is this? A coffee-shop Wi-Fi in the Plateau, a fourteen-person notarial firm in Québec City, a multi-site logistics outfit with offices in Mississauga and Calgary, and a hospital in Sherbrooke are four wildly different defensive problems. They share a Transmission Control Protocol / Internet Protocol (TCP/IP) stack and almost nothing else.

Network types you must recognise by name and threat surface:

  • Personal Area Network (PAN) — Bluetooth headphones, a smartwatch, the laptop you brought to class. Range: roughly the width of a human body. Threat surface: pairing attacks, Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) sniffing, evil-twin pairings.
  • Local Area Network (LAN) — everything inside a single physical site. Threat surface: ARP poisoning, rogue Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol (DHCP) servers, lateral movement.
  • Campus Area Network (CAN) — several LANs on contiguous private land (a university, a corporate campus, a hospital). Threat surface: same as LAN, multiplied by the number of buildings and the patience of the cleaning staff who prop fire doors open.
  • Metropolitan Area Network (MAN) — a city-scale interconnect, usually run by a single carrier or municipality. Threat surface: carrier-level misconfiguration, Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) leaks, physical-cable cuts.
  • Wide Area Network (WAN) — multi-city, multi-country. Multiprotocol Label Switching (MPLS), Software-Defined WAN (SD-WAN), site-to-site Virtual Private Network (VPN). Threat surface: transit-provider compromise, tunnel mis-keying, asymmetric routing.
  • Storage Area Network (SAN) — a dedicated network whose only job is to carry block-storage traffic to and from servers (Fibre Channel or Internet Small Computer Systems Interface, iSCSI). Threat surface: if compromised, an attacker has the disks — not the files, the disks.
  • VPN — Virtual Private Network — not a place, a tunnel. Connects any two of the above over an untrusted carrier. Threat surface: the trust model itself — once you are inside the tunnel, what stops you from going anywhere?

A correction that students get wrong on the exam, every year: a VLAN is not in this list. A Virtual LAN (VLAN) is a segmentation construct inside a single physical LAN — not a type of network. We come back to it in Chapter 6.

Topology shapes — taught in failure-mode order, not chronological order. The textbook order (bus → ring → star → mesh → tree → hybrid) is historically honest and pedagogically misleading. The question that matters in a security class is not "what does it look like" but "what breaks when one link goes down":

  • Bus — single shared backbone. Failure mode: one cable cut takes down the whole network. Security implication: every host sees every other host's traffic, so passive sniffing is trivial. Almost no modern network uses bus, but the legacy serial-line industrial systems you will meet later in the course sometimes still do.
  • Ring — each device connects to two neighbours, traffic flows one direction (or both, in dual-ring). Failure mode: one device down breaks the ring (unless dual-ring). Still seen in some carrier MAN deployments running SONET — Synchronous Optical Networking.
  • Star — every device connects to a central switch or hub. Failure mode: the switch is the single point of failure; lose the switch, lose everything. This is what 99 % of office LANs actually are.
  • Mesh — every node is connected to many others, often all others. Failure mode: survives almost any single link failure; the cost is wiring, routing complexity, and operational expense. Used in carrier cores and Wi-Fi mesh systems.
  • Tree — a hierarchy of stars: access switches feeding distribution switches feeding a core. Failure mode: the higher you cut, the more you take down. This is the real shape of most enterprise networks once you look past the first cabinet.
  • Hybrid — the honest label for anything in production. A star at the access layer, a partial mesh at the core, a ring at the metro, a bus on the serial-line PLC (Programmable Logic Controller) in the basement. Real networks are hybrids; calling them anything else is a lie of convenience.

A real-world failure cited verbatim in the source material: a regional Québec credit union lost network across three branches for six hours when a single fibre cut occurred outside Drummondville. Why? The carrier had marketed the service as "ring-protected" but had never actually built the second strand. The shape was advertised as a ring; the physical reality was a bus. The architectural decision that caused the outage was trusting the carrier's marketing language instead of asking for the physical-circuit diagram. The shape decides the failure mode — write that on the inside of your eyelids.

▾ Chapter 6 — Segmentation

(Source: Days 03–04 — Network Topology, Lecture 1, Section 3)

A flat network is one in which every host can reach every other host directly at Layer 2 (the data-link layer, where Media Access Control — MAC — addresses live) and Layer 3 (the network layer, where IP addresses live). It is the default state of any LAN that nobody has bothered to segment. The empirical observation that drives this entire chapter: the median time from initial compromise to lateral movement is measured in minutes, not days. By the time the Security Operations Centre (SOC) gets the first alert, the attacker is already inside the crown jewels.

Three segmentation primitives you must internalise:

  • Subnetting — split a single IP range into smaller ranges. A router has to decide whether to forward packets between them — and routers can also drop packets. That is where the security comes from.
  • VLAN — Virtual LAN — split a single physical switch into multiple logical Layer-2 broadcast domains. Hosts on different VLANs cannot talk to each other even if they are plugged into the same switch, unless a router (or a Layer-3 switch) is configured to allow it.
  • Microsegmentation — firewall rules at the workload level, not the network level. The web server can talk to the database server on port 5432 and nothing else, regardless of whether they sit on the same VLAN, same subnet, or even the same physical box. This is what Zero Trust networks actually deliver in practice (see Chapter 7).

The Hamilton accounting-firm incident. A thirty-five-employee accounting firm in Hamilton, Ontario, was hit with ransomware in 2024. The initial compromise was a phishing email opened by a junior bookkeeper. Within eighteen minutes, the malware had encrypted (a) the bookkeeper's workstation, (b) the partner's workstation across the office, (c) the on-premises file server, and (d) the QuickBooks server. Everything was on 192.168.1.0/24 — one subnet, one broadcast domain, no internal firewalling. The simplest VLAN split that would have helped:

VLAN ID Subnet What lives there
Users 10 192.168.10.0/24 Workstations (bookkeepers, partners)
Servers 20 192.168.20.0/24 File server, QuickBooks server, print server
Management 30 192.168.30.0/24 Switch and router management interfaces
Guest 40 192.168.40.0/24 Visitor Wi-Fi, no LAN access

Combined with one inter-VLAN Access Control List (ACL) — "users may reach servers only on Server Message Block (SMB, port 445), HTTPS (port 443), and QuickBooks (port 8019); servers may not initiate connections to users" — the lateral spread workstation-to-workstation and file-server-to-QuickBooks would have been blocked.

The lesson is not that VLANs are a silver bullet. The lesson is that the bullet you fired was a /24, and that is on you.

▾ Chapter 7 — Defensible architecture: Defense in Depth and Zero Trust

(Source: Days 03–04 — Network Topology, Lecture 1, Section 4)

SANS — SysAdmin, Audit, Network, Security has been teaching the Defense-in-Depth model since the 1990s, and it survives because it is operationally honest: it admits that any one control will fail, so you plan for the failure by stacking controls. The seven canonical layers (some sources collapse two, others split one; the spirit is the same):

Layer What lives here Example control
1 — Policies & Awareness The human layer Acceptable-use policy, phishing training
2 — Physical Locks, doors, cameras Badge readers, mantraps
3 — Perimeter The edge Firewall, Intrusion Prevention System (IPS)
4 — Internal Network LAN-side VLANs, Network Access Control (NAC), 802.1X
5 — Host OS-level Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR), patching, antivirus
6 — Application App-layer Web Application Firewall (WAF), input validation
7 — Data The crown jewels Encryption at rest, Data Loss Prevention (DLP)

Defense in Depth is what you draw when you want to explain the architecture to a Chief Executive Officer (CEO). It is the layered cake. Zero Trust is the answer to the question "what if the perimeter is already inside out?" In 2020 NIST published Special Publication 800-207 — Zero Trust Architecture. Its central thesis is one line: "Never trust, always verify." In practice that means:

  • The fact that a packet came from inside the LAN is not a credential.
  • The fact that a user logged in this morning is not ongoing authentication.
  • The fact that a laptop is managed is not a green light without a device-posture check.

Three load-bearing components of any Zero Trust implementation:

  • Policy Decision Point (PDP) — the brain that says yes or no.
  • Policy Enforcement Point (PEP) — the gate that does what the brain says.
  • Identity and device-posture signals — without them, the brain is guessing.

A common confusion to clear up explicitly: Zero Trust is not the death of Defense in Depth. It is a particular way of arranging the inner layers of DiD so that the "internal network" layer no longer provides implicit trust.

The Sherbrooke hospital example. A 220-bed hospital with about six hundred endpoint devices, an on-premises Electronic Health Record (EHR) system, regulated under PHIPA — Personal Health Information Protection Act (Ontario) and Québec's Law 25, 24/7 operations (so patching windows are tight), and no money for a green-field rebuild. The defensible-architecture design from the source material:

Layer Control for the EHR dataset
Policies & Awareness Annual PHIPA refresher; phishing simulations every quarter; "no shared accounts" enforced
Physical Server room locked; CCTV; visitor sign-in; no USB ports on clinical workstations
Perimeter Next-Generation Firewall (NGFW) with explicit deny-by-default; geo-blocking; egress filtering
Internal Network EHR servers on a dedicated VLAN; only clinical workstations may reach them; no flat /16
Host EDR on every clinical workstation; auto-patch within 14 days non-critical, 48 h critical
Application EHR access mediated by Single Sign-On (SSO); MFA for all administrative accounts; full audit logging
Data Database encrypted at rest with keys held in a Hardware Security Module (HSM); encrypted backups off-site; immutable for 14 days

A Zero Trust overlay sits on top: a small Network Policy Server (NPS) cluster acts as the PDP, consuming signals from Active Directory (AD), Microsoft Defender for Endpoint (device posture), and the badge-reader system (physical presence). The PEP is the EHR application's own access-gateway, which calls the PDP on every request — not just at login. A clinician who has badged out of the building or whose laptop has fallen behind on EDR signatures gets a "stale-posture" denial even if their session is still technically valid.

The Defensible-Architecture Checklist — print it, tape it to the back of your laptop:

  1. What type of network is this? (LAN / WAN / MAN / SAN / …)
  2. What topology is it actually? (Star, tree, hybrid — never trust the label.)
  3. How many broadcast domains? (One is almost always wrong.)
  4. Where is the boundary between user and server traffic?
  5. Where is the boundary between Information Technology (IT) and Operational Technology (OT), if any?
  6. Is administrative access on its own segment?
  7. Is guest access physically and logically separated?
  8. Is there a single point of failure — physical, logical, human?
  9. Which DiD layers are present, and which are not?
  10. Are any Zero Trust signals actually being collected and acted on?
▾ Chapter 8 — Reading a network diagram for security

(Source: Days 03–04 — Network Topology, Lecture 2, Sections 1–4)

Every real network has at least three diagrams, not one:

  • Physical — what is wired to what (cables, switch ports, host Network Interface Cards — NICs).
  • Logical — what speaks to what at Layer 3 (subnets, gateways, routes).
  • Trust — what is allowed to talk to what at Layer 4 and above. The security map.

Most production networks are documented by a single diagram, somewhere on a shared drive, last updated by a person who left the company in 2019. The security questions live almost entirely in the trust diagram — but the trust diagram only makes sense when you can place it on top of the other two. The conflation of physical and logical produces two predictable failures: physical-only diagrams miss every Layer-3 misconfiguration (the cables are perfect, the network is broken); logical-only diagrams miss every Layer-1 single point of failure (the routes are perfect, one cleaner with a vacuum cleaner takes the building down).

The five elements every good diagram must contain (implicitly assumed by both the Cisco Reference Network Architecture documentation and the NIST SP 800-53 Rev. 5 control catalogue):

  • A title block — what network, what version, what date, what author. Without this, the diagram is folklore.
  • A legend — every line style, every shape, every colour. Without a legend, every reader invents their own.
  • Addressing — at least one IP per subnet, gateway, and exit interface. Without addressing, the diagram is decorative.
  • Trust zones — coloured or labelled regions for "Internet", "DMZ", "Internal", "Restricted". Without zones, every reader has to re-derive the security boundary from scratch.
  • A scope note — what is not on this diagram (because no diagram contains everything, and pretending it does is the lie that buries us).

The ten-second triage. When a diagram lands on your desk, triage in this order — every time:

  1. Where is the Internet?
  2. Where is the boundary firewall?
  3. Where is the Demilitarised Zone (DMZ)?
  4. Where is administrative access? (Same subnet as users = bad. Separate = good.)
  5. Where is the data? (Flagged separately from compute, or mixed in?)

If any of these is missing, you have a finding before you have read a single label.

The next thirty seconds. Count the broadcast domains (one = bad; many = depends). Look for the words "trust" and "untrust" — they should appear on a real diagram even if zones are unlabelled. Look for redundancy: is there a single line you can erase that disconnects something critical? Look for the cleaner — where in this building does the cleaner have unsupervised access, and are any network jacks live there? Look for the printer — printers are the most-forgotten persistent foothold in any enterprise.

The twelve architectural smells — the back-of-the-T-shirt findings you will spot in real diagrams:

# Smell What it suggests
1 One big subnet No segmentation, lateral movement is free
2 "Firewall" with no zone labels The firewall is a router with a sticker
3 Public-facing service with no DMZ Compromised service = inside the LAN

▾ Chapter 9 — The Day-05 toolset: packets, scans, polls, alerts

(Source: Days 05–06 — Installation and Configuration of Monitoring Tools, Lecture 1)

Network monitoring is not one job — it is at least four overlapping jobs, and the classic free tools were each built for a different one. The cheat sheet you must memorise:

Job to be done What you are actually looking at Classic free tool
Packet analysis — what bytes crossed this wire? Frames, packets, payloads Wireshark
Network discovery — what hosts are alive, what services do they run? Hosts, ports, banners Nmap / Zenmap
Performance graphing — how busy was this interface last Tuesday at 3 a.m.? Time-series of SNMP counters Cacti
Availability monitoring — is the database server still alive? Up / down / degraded states Nagios
Polling protocol — how do tools above ask devices for status? UDP 161/162 conversations SNMP (Simple Network Management Protocol), defined in RFC 1157
Log shipping protocol — how does a device tell us what just happened? UDP/TCP 514 messages syslog, defined in RFC 5424
Deep network security monitoring — was that conversation suspicious? Connection logs, file extracts, scripts Zeek
Intrusion detection / prevention — does this packet match a known bad pattern? Signature alerts Suricata

If you remember only one thing from this chapter: packets, metrics, logs and alerts are four different food groups. A healthy monitoring diet has some of all four.

Wireshark. Gerald Combs released it as Ethereal in 1998; a trademark dispute in 2006 forced the rename. Under the hood it leans on libpcap (or Npcap on Windows) to put your Network Interface Card (NIC) into promiscuous mode and copy frames as they arrive. The real power lives in three places: display filters (http.request and ip.addr == 10.0.0.5), Statistics → Conversations, and Follow → TCP Stream. The capture-filter syntax is Berkeley Packet Filter (BPF); the display-filter syntax is Wireshark's own. Mixing them up makes the screen go blank — a rite of passage. A field case from the source material: a junior analyst at a Toronto credit union finds the SNMP community string public in clear text by filtering udp port 161 while a colleague triggers a print job — exactly the kind of finding that gets a printer upgraded to SNMPv3 the next morning.

Nmap and Zenmap. Written by Gordon "Fyodor" Lyon in 1997, Nmap is the de-facto standard for network discovery. Zenmap is its cross-platform GUI. A few invocations to know by heart:

  • nmap -sn 10.20.10.0/24 — ping sweep, no port scan
  • nmap -sV 10.20.10.5 — service-version detection
  • nmap -O 10.20.10.5 — OS fingerprinting (needs root)
  • nmap -p- -T4 10.20.10.5 — scan all 65,535 TCP ports
  • nmap --script vuln 10.20.10.5 — run the Nmap Scripting Engine (NSE) vulnerability scripts

Field case: a Vancouver dental clinic's new IT manager inherits an undocumented network and uses nmap -sV -oX inventory.xml to produce a machine-readable inventory of every host and service.

Cacti. Born in 2001 from Ian Berry's frustration with the raw RRDtool (Round-Robin Database tool) command-line. Cacti polls SNMP counters at a fixed interval (five minutes by default) and draws time-series graphs. Its sweet spot is performance trending — "was the link saturated last Tuesday at 16:00?" — not real-time alerting. Field case: a regional Internet Service Provider (ISP) in New Brunswick uses Cacti to confirm the 1 Gbps uplink saturates during the after-school rush.

Nagios. Ethan Galstad released NetSaint in 1999; renamed Nagios in 2002 after a trademark dispute. Nagios is the canonical availability monitor — it asks "is this service up?" every minute and pages someone if the answer changes. The notable forks: Icinga, Naemon, and Shinken. Most useful trivia for the exam: a Nagios plugin is just an executable that returns one of four exit codes — 0 OK, 1 WARNING, 2 CRITICAL, 3 UNKNOWN — plus a single line of output to standard out. Writing your own Nagios check is genuinely trivial; most plugins are twenty-line scripts.

SNMP. RFC 1157, 1990. Three versions still in the wild: v1 and v2c send community strings in clear text (and public is still the most common one, sadly); v3 adds authentication and encryption, and is the only version a defender should accept on a production network. The two operations you must recognise: GET (manager asks agent) and TRAP (agent volunteers an alert). Both use User Datagram Protocol (UDP) by default — port 161 for GET, port 162 for TRAP. Data is addressed by Object Identifier (OID) values catalogued in Management Information Base (MIB) files.

syslog. Defined modernly in RFC 5424 (the older RFC 3164 BSD format is still pervasive in the wild). Eight severity levels, numbered 0 (Emergency) through 7 (Debug); a handful of facility codes (auth, daemon, kern, local0–7, and so on). UDP/TCP 514. On modern Linux the daemon is usually rsyslog; on macOS Apple's unified logging; on Windows the Event Log subsystem speaks a different protocol entirely and needs an agent to convert to syslog. A field case from the source material: a small Québec credit union must keep one year of authentication logs to comply with OSFI Guideline B-13 — central rsyslog is the cheapest way to meet that obligation.

Zeek (formerly Bro). Vern Paxson, 1995, Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory. Zeek is not a signature scanner — it is a traffic understanding engine that produces structured logs (conn.log, dns.log, http.log, ssl.log, files.log, and dozens more) describing every conversation on a wire. The output is what you feed into your SIEM. Pairs naturally with Suricata.

Suricata. Open Information Security Foundation, 2009. Suricata is the modern Network-based Intrusion Detection / Prevention System (NIDS / NIPS) — it matches packets against signature rules (the canonical free ruleset is Emerging Threats). Suricata also produces Zeek-style JSON event logs as a side-benefit. The shorthand to remember: Zeek understands traffic; Suricata pattern-matches traffic.

▾ Chapter 10 — The Day-06 toolset: SIEMs, HIDS, and central correlation

(Source: Days 05–06 — Installation and Configuration of Monitoring Tools, Lecture 2)

Day-05 tools are the plumbing — how to capture, scan, poll, log. Day-06 tools are the brains — they ingest everything, correlate it across sources, and turn raw signals into actionable security events.

Splunk. Founded in 2003 by Michael Baum, Rob Das, and Erik Swan in San Francisco. The elevator pitch was Google for log files. In March 2024 Cisco completed its ≈ 28 B USD acquisition of Splunk. For the classroom the important point: Splunk Enterprise has a free tier (Splunk Free) capped at 500 MB/day of indexing — far more than enough for a lab. Three roles you will hear over and over:

  • Forwarder — agent on each endpoint that ships data
  • Indexer — where the data lives, on disk, indexed for search
  • Search Head — where you query

In a small lab all three run on the same VM (Virtual Machine). Queries are written in SPL — Search Processing Language:

index=main sourcetype=linux_secure "Failed password"
| stats count by user, src_ip
| sort -count

That single line ranks the top brute-forcers on your SSH-exposed servers — exactly the kind of question that took hours of grep | awk | sort before Splunk existed. A field case from the source material: a Québec ski resort's IT director uses index=windows EventCode=4624 _time>="01/04/2026:02:00:00" _time<="01/04/2026:03:00:00" to identify a 02:14 Saturday-morning login on the snow-grooming control system. The account had been phished a week earlier.

Wazuh. Forked from OSSEC in 2015 by a Spanish team led by Santiago Bassett. Today Wazuh is a full open-source security platform bundling HIDS (Host-based Intrusion Detection System), vulnerability detection, File Integrity Monitoring (FIM), configuration assessment, and a SIEM-style web UI built on OpenSearch. Three core components plus the agents:

  • Wazuh server — analyses events
  • Wazuh indexer — OpenSearch under the hood, stores events
  • Wazuh dashboard — the web UI
  • Wazuh agents — on every monitored endpoint

Wazuh ships hundreds of out-of-the-box rules: failed-login storms, Linux useradd events, EDR-style behavioural alerts, MITRE ATT&CK mappings, and integrations with VirusTotal, AbuseIPDB, and other threat-intel feeds. Most labs start with level >= 10 alerts and tune downward. Custom rules go in /var/ossec/etc/rules/local_rules.xml; user rule IDs start at 100000 to avoid collisions with the built-ins.

A field case from the source material: a Toronto independent bookstore detects an after-hours USB-drive insertion on a Point-Of-Sale (POS) machine via a Wazuh rule on Sysmon Event ID 6416 ("a new external device was recognised by the system"). The IT consultant remotes in, isolates the machine, and hands the drive to the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) cybercrime team.

ELK / OpenSearch stack. Elastic launched open-source Elasticsearch in 2010, then added Logstash and Kibana — the famous ELK acronym. After a licence change in 2021, AWS forked Elasticsearch into OpenSearch, which is now the truly open variant. ELK / OpenSearch is the most common DIY (do-it-yourself) SIEM substrate: Logstash or Filebeat ships logs in, Elasticsearch / OpenSearch stores them, Kibana / OpenSearch Dashboards visualises them.

Graylog. Open-source SIEM by Graylog Inc., under the hood it uses OpenSearch for storage and MongoDB for configuration. Often chosen by teams who want SIEM functionality with a friendlier UI than raw Kibana.

Security Onion. A free Linux distribution by Doug Burks that bundles Suricata + Zeek + Wazuh + ELK into a single installer. If you want a "SOC in a box" for the lab, this is it. Project home: securityonion.net.

TheHive. A free incident-response case-management platform by Strangebee. Stores investigations, observables, and tasks; integrates with MISP — Malware Information Sharing Platform for threat intelligence. Outside the strict scope of Day-06 lecture, but appears in the source material as a "bonus" tool you should at least recognise on the exam.

▾ Case Study 1 — The Montréal dental clinic, revisited from a defender's chair

(Anchored in Day 01 — Cybersecurity Fundamentals, Lecture 1, §1.2.1)

The situation. You have just been hired as the first dedicated IT person for a small dental clinic in Plateau-Mont-Royal, Montréal. On your second day, the practice manager mentions in passing that the previous receptionist account had Full Control on the \\CLINIC-SRV\Patients share "because it was easier than fixing it later." She is leaving the practice on Friday. Her stolen laptop, two months later, ends up on a flea market.

What do you do today?

Model answer. The single sentence that frames this is: the leak that is about to happen is a Confidentiality breach caused by a violation of least privilege, and the fix has to land before Friday. Concretely, this morning you (a) enumerate share permissions with PowerShell, using Get-SmbShareAccess -Name Patients — exactly the audit pattern in the source material; (b) document every "Full Control" grant on a non-admin account; (c) draft a replacement permission scheme using Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) — receptionists get only the List + Read they need to print appointment lists, hygienists get scoped Read on their own patients, and full clinical staff get Read+Write on patient records; (d) get the practice manager's sign-off on the new model in writing (you are about to remove access that has been granted by a human, and you want the paper trail); (e) implement the change Thursday evening with a tested rollback; (f) on Friday morning, before the departing receptionist hands in her badge, you disable her account and immediately rotate the credentials on every service account she ever touched. The stolen-laptop scenario — the punchline of the source — becomes a non-event because the credentials it carries no longer authorise anything.

A subtle bonus point if you spotted it: you also enable Windows file-share auditing (auditpol /set /subcategory:"File Share" /success:enable /failure:enable) so that when the next mistake is made, you have a forensic trail. Defence-in-depth at the host layer.

▾ Case Study 2 — A Cisco config silently changed

(Anchored in Day 01 — Cybersecurity Fundamentals, Lecture 1, §1.2.2)

The situation. You manage a single Cisco router for a small Ottawa co-working space. Every Sunday at 22:00 a cron job on a Linux box backs up router.cfg and computes its SHA-256 hash. Monday morning, your monitoring tool screams: the new hash differs from last week's hash, but no change-control ticket exists. Walk me through how you respond, and how you would have caught this earlier.

Model answer. Two things matter on Monday morning: what changed, and who changed it. For what, you diff Sunday's config against Monday's — diff router.cfg.sunday router.cfg.monday. The output reveals a single new line: permit any any somewhere it should not be. For who, you pull the router's show login on-failure and show users and you cross-reference with the SSH log on the jump-host. Investigation reveals a junior tech who "wanted to test something" at 03:17 Sunday morning. The Integrity violation is real, but it was caught only because the hash changed.

The deeper question — how to catch this earlier — has three parts. First, you front your router with a configuration-management system (RANCID, Oxidized, or Cisco Prime) that polls the running config every fifteen minutes, not weekly. Second, you enforce a four-eyes approval rule on enable-mode changes by routing all admin SSH through a jump-host that records every keystroke (TACACS+ accounting on the router, full session recording on the jump-host). Third — and this is the harder cultural fix — you make the after-hours "I just wanted to test something" pattern a fireable offence, communicated explicitly, before the next junior tech tries it. The avalanche effect of SHA-256 caught one byte. Process is what stops the next change from happening at all.

▾ Case Study 3 — A 99.9 % SLO and a 50 GB disk

(Anchored in Day 01 — Cybersecurity Fundamentals, Lecture 1, §1.5)

The situation. A small Calgary online bookshop targets 99.9 % availability for its checkout page. Their finance director, after reading about a recent SSH brute-force incident, has decreed that all SSH authentication logs must be kept for 365 days "to be safe." The jump-host has a 50 GB system disk. The logs grow at 400 MB/day. The director will be in the meeting at 14:00 today. Build the case that gets her to revise the policy.

Model answer. This is a Confidentiality-driven policy collapsing into an Availability disaster, and the maths is what you bring to the meeting. At 400 MB × 365 = 146 GB, the disk fills in 125 days. Once the disk is full, the SSH daemon stops logging, then stops accepting connections (syslog blocks, pam_unix blocks, the cascading-failure pattern is well-documented). The jump-host goes offline. The 99.9 % Availability target — which permits only 8 h 45 min of downtime per year — is blown by a single day of disk-full. The very policy intended to increase defence has just destroyed it.

The counter-proposal you walk into the 14:00 meeting with: 90 days hot, 9 months cold. Keep ninety days on the jump-host's local disk (≈ 36 GB, leaving comfortable headroom for the OS and unexpected log spikes), and ship the daily rotation to an object-storage bucket (AWS S3 — Simple Storage Service, or any S3-compatible alternative) configured with lifecycle policies that move logs to a cheap tier after thirty days and delete after 365. Cost: roughly €5/month versus a full disk failure. You frame it explicitly: "we are not reducing retention; we are moving most of it to storage that doesn't take the jump-host down with it." The director signs off. You document the trade-off in the security-architecture register and move on.

▾ Case Study 4 — The flat /24 ransomware incident

(Anchored in Days 03–04 — Network Topology, §3.3)

The situation. A friend asks you to help her family's thirty-five-person accounting firm in Hamilton, Ontario, recover from a ransomware incident. Initial compromise: a phishing email opened by a junior bookkeeper. Within eighteen minutes the malware encrypted the bookkeeper's workstation, the partner's workstation across the office, the file server, and the QuickBooks server. Everything sat on 192.168.1.0/24. You are advising on the rebuild.

Model answer. The reason this incident progressed eighteen-minutes-end-to-end is that the network was flat. Backup recovery first (and only if backups exist and are tested and are offline — three conditions; if any is missing you advise calling Coveware or a similar negotiator rather than letting the firm pay attackers directly). Then the rebuild.

You propose four VLANs — the canonical split from the source material. Users on 192.168.10.0/24, Servers on 192.168.20.0/24, Management on 192.168.30.0/24, Guest Wi-Fi on 192.168.40.0/24. The single inter-VLAN Access Control List (ACL) reads in plain English: "Users may reach Servers only on SMB (port 445), HTTPS (port 443), and QuickBooks (port 8019). Servers may not initiate connections to Users. Guest cannot reach anything but the Internet. Management is reachable only from a single workstation pinned in the Management VLAN by Media Access Control (MAC) address."

You then add three controls the source material does not mention explicitly but that any responsible advisor would: (1) Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) on every account, including the QuickBooks server's local admin; (2) an EDR agent on every workstation, sending alerts to a Wazuh or Splunk Free instance you stand up yourself for the firm; (3) tested, off-site, immutable backups of the file server and QuickBooks — verified by an actual restore exercise the following Saturday morning, not just by a green checkmark in the backup software's UI.

The lesson you write at the bottom of your report — verbatim from the source material because nobody else has said it better: "VLANs are not a silver bullet. The bullet you fired was a /24, and that is on you."

▾ Case Study 5 — Reading the dental-chain diagram cold

(Anchored in Days 03–04 — Network Topology, §4.2)

The situation. A forty-person dental chain — four clinics plus a head office — has handed you their 2019 network diagram for an architectural review. Each clinic shows: a single router/firewall, a single switch, eight staff workstations, two X-ray imaging machines on a proprietary OS with no patching support, a guest Wi-Fi shared with patients, and a printer. The head office shows the Electronic Medical Records (EMR) server, the billing application, and the IT admin's workstation. Wireless networks are on the same VLAN as wired. There is no DMZ. The patient portal is hosted at head office on the same VLAN as the EMR. The diagram is dated 2019; the team viewing it is from 2024. Apply the twelve-smell checklist and pick your top three findings to brief the CEO.

Model answer. Walking the smell checklist in order, you tick:

# Smell Present?
1 One big subnet ✔ — each clinic is a flat /24
3 Public-facing service with no DMZ ✔ — patient portal hosted alongside EMR
4 Admin interface on user VLAN ✔ — IT admin workstation on same VLAN as EMR
6 IoT devices on user network ✔ — X-ray machines (unpatched proprietary OS) on staff VLAN
7 Wireless on the same VLAN as wired ✔ — patient Wi-Fi can reach the EMR
10 No out-of-band management ✔ — if VPN drops, no fallback path
12 Diagram older than the team viewing it ✔ — 2019 diagram, 2024 team

Seven of twelve. Bad — and, as the source material notes, typical for the sector.

The CEO briefing distils to three findings. Critical: the patient portal is on the same broadcast domain as the EMR; an attacker who compromises the portal is one Layer-2 hop away from the patient database — exactly the path PHIPA and Québec Law 25 are designed to prevent. High: the X-ray machines run an unpatched proprietary OS and sit on the staff VLAN; one of them is a single phishing email away from being the foothold that pivots into the staff workstations. High: patient Wi-Fi is on the staff VLAN, so any guest with a $30 USB Wi-Fi adapter has Layer-2 reachability to the EMR. You present these three first, with their proposed remediations costed (three new VLAN switches, one NGFW with a DMZ zone, an IoT-segment Wi-Fi controller), and you put the remaining four smells in the appendix.

The art is restraint. Seven findings is a panic; three findings is a plan.

▾ Case Study 6 — The Toronto credit-union printer

(Anchored in Days 05–06 — Installation and Configuration of Monitoring Tools, Tool 1)

The situation. You are a Tier-1 SOC analyst at a Toronto-based credit union. A senior analyst messages you on Slack: "The auditor flagged our office printer as a finding. Can you confirm whether the SNMP community string is in clear text on the wire? Reply by lunch." You have Wireshark on your laptop and a network jack in the same VLAN as the printer. Walk through how you confirm or refute the finding.

Model answer. Five steps, none of which involves panicking.

(1) Authorisation. You confirm in writing — Slack message or email — that the senior analyst has authority to instruct you to capture on the corporate VLAN. This is not bureaucracy; this is the difference between a Security Analyst and a defendant under the Canadian Criminal Code s.342.1. The two-minute paper trail will protect you.

(2) Capture. You launch Wireshark, select the wired interface on the printer's VLAN, and apply the capture filter udp port 161 (Berkeley Packet Filter — BPF — syntax). Then you press the blue shark-fin and walk back to your desk.

(3) Stimulus. You print a test page. Modern multifunction printers report their status via SNMP after almost every state change; the test print is enough to produce traffic. You give it sixty seconds.

(4) Analysis. You stop the capture, switch to the display filter snmp (Wireshark's own filter syntax, not BPF — a common confusion), and double-click the first packet. You expand the SNMP tree. If you see community: public (or any other string) in plain ASCII, the answer is unambiguous: the printer is running SNMPv1 or SNMPv2c and the community string is on the wire in clear text, exactly as predicted by RFC 1157. You take a screenshot.

(5) Report. Three sentences to the senior analyst by lunch: "Finding confirmed. Capture attached. Recommend upgrade to SNMPv3 with authPriv on the device, then restrict UDP/161 to the Network Management Station (NMS) by ACL on the switch." You attach the screenshot, not the raw .pcapng (which contains other traffic you are not authorised to share even internally), and you note the timestamp range of the capture.

The whole investigation takes twenty minutes. The finding is not impressive on its own; the impressive part is that you scoped the work, captured legally, analysed deterministically, and reported succinctly — which is exactly what an SOC career looks like.

▾ Case Study 7 — The Vancouver dental clinic with no inventory

(Anchored in Days 05–06 — Installation and Configuration of Monitoring Tools, Tool 2)

The situation. A dental clinic in Vancouver has just hired you as their first IT manager. The previous administrator left no documentation. You need to produce, by end of week, an authoritative list of every device on the office subnet 10.50.0.0/24 and the services they expose. The clinic's router log shows about thirty active hosts. You have a Linux laptop and Nmap. How do you build the inventory, what risks are you taking, and what do you do with the result?

Model answer. Three Nmap invocations form the spine of the answer.

First, a quiet ping sweep to enumerate live hosts:

nmap -sn -PE -PP -PS22,80,443 -PA22,80,443 -oA discovery 10.50.0.0/24

This sends a mix of ICMP (Internet Control Message Protocol) Echo, ICMP Timestamp, TCP SYN to common ports, and TCP ACK — covering the firewall configurations under which one or another probe will be dropped. The -oA flag emits all three output formats (normal, grepable, XML) under one prefix; the XML is the one you will parse later.

Second, a service-version scan of every responsive host:

nmap -sV -O --top-ports 1000 -oA inventory --reason -iL discovery_live.txt

-sV does banner/version detection; -O does OS fingerprinting (requires root). --top-ports 1000 keeps the scan under twenty minutes per host. --reason records why Nmap concluded each port was open — useful when the dental software vendor's tech support inevitably argues with your findings.

Third, a targeted vulnerability scan of any host running a service version Nmap recognises:

nmap --script vuln -iL inventory_live.txt -oA vulnscan

The Nmap Scripting Engine (NSE) vulnerability scripts are not a substitute for a real vulnerability scanner like OpenVAS or Nessus, but they catch the headline-grade issues (SMBGhost, Eternal-Blue still living on a 2017 Windows server, default credentials on an embedded device).

The risks. You will trigger the dental imaging software's tamper-protection at least once; you will receive a worried call from a hygienist whose appointment-scheduling laptop briefly slowed down; you may light up the alerting on the clinic's internet service provider router (some ISPs interpret an internal scan as a compromised host). Mitigate all three by (a) announcing the scan window in writing the day before, (b) keeping the scan to lunch hours, (c) using -T3 (the default "normal" timing) rather than -T4 or -T5, even though it is slower.

What you do with the result. You produce three artefacts: a CSV inventory (parsed from the XML), a network diagram in draw.io (the source material's recommended tool), and an Architectural Risk Register listing every host whose OS is past end-of-life, every default credential found, and every clear-text protocol (Telnet, FTP, SNMPv1) still in active use. The Risk Register becomes the input to the next week's remediation plan — and to your first Course Procedure Manual chapter for this clinic.

▾ Case Study 8 — The Halifax classroom switches dying of heat

(Anchored in Days 05–06 — Installation and Configuration of Monitoring Tools, Tool 5)

The situation. A Halifax community college has lost three classroom switches in one summer — all overheated because a well-meaning custodian closed the wiring-closet door every Friday evening. The Director of IT asks you to instrument the switches so the network team is alerted the next time the closet temperature climbs above 65 °C. You have SNMP on the switches and a Windows-based Network Management Station (NMS). Walk through your design.

Model answer. The architecture is a five-minute polling loop from the NMS to each switch, comparing a vendor-specific Object Identifier (OID) against a threshold and emailing the network team on breach.

(1) Identify the OID. Every switch vendor publishes a Management Information Base (MIB) file describing its objects. Cisco's CISCO-ENVMON-MIB exposes ciscoEnvMonTemperatureValue as the per-sensor temperature reading; HP's HP-ICF-CHASSIS has its own variant; Aruba and Juniper similar. You walk the MIB once with snmpwalk -v3 -u monuser -l authPriv -a SHA -A '<authpw>' -x AES -X '<privpw>' switch01 1.3.6.1.4.1.9.9.13.1.3 to confirm the actual OID and the value scale for this switch model. Vendor MIB documentation lies often enough that you verify.

(2) Configure SNMPv3. SNMPv1 and v2c are not acceptable on production network gear — both send the community string in clear text. You configure each switch with an SNMPv3 user (monuser), an authentication credential (SHA — Secure Hash Algorithm), and a privacy credential (AES — Advanced Encryption Standard). Restrict the SNMP access list to the NMS IP only.

(3) Poll from the NMS. A PowerShell script on the Windows NMS runs every five minutes (Task Scheduler), pulls the temperature OID from every switch, and emails the network team when any value exceeds 65 °C. The source material shows exactly this script pattern. Logging goes to a file so you have a trend you can later import into Cacti for visual graphing.

(4) Acceptance test. You walk into the wiring closet on a Friday afternoon, close the door, watch the NMS log, and verify the alert email arrives. You then prop the door open with the doorstop you installed earlier, and email the custodian with a friendly note explaining why the closet door must remain open. The fix you have just deployed catches the symptom (heat); the cause (a custodian following building-safety policy without knowing the IT consequence) is solved by the email and a physical sign on the door.

Honourable mention. A senior engineer would extend the same script to log the readings into Cacti or Zabbix so the network team can see the trend, not just the breach. The breach is the symptom; the trend is the diagnostic.

▾ Case Study 9 — The Québec ski-resort 02:14 login

(Anchored in Days 05–06 — Installation and Configuration of Monitoring Tools, Tool 9)

The situation. You are the IT director of a regional ski resort in the Laurentides. On Sunday morning the on-call snow-grooming supervisor mentions, casually, that the snow-grooming control system showed someone logged in at 02:14 the previous night — when nobody should have been on shift. The system forwards Windows Event Logs to a Splunk Free instance you set up in October. Walk through the investigation.

Model answer. Splunk is the right tool because the question — "who logged in, when, from where?" — is exactly the centralised-search use case. You log into Splunk Web and write the query:

index=windows EventCode=4624 
  _time>="04/01/2026:02:00:00" _time<="04/01/2026:03:00:00"

Event Code 4624 is the Windows Security log entry for "an account was successfully logged on" — the canonical first thing to look for. The time window is narrow to keep the result set human-readable.

Three results return. One of them is the user t.lebel from IP 10.10.20.34 — a workstation in the maintenance shed. You pivot:

index=windows user=t.lebel _time>="03/25/2026:00:00:00"
| stats count by EventCode, src_ip, _time
| sort _time

This shows every t.lebel login for the past week. A clear pattern emerges: logins from the maintenance-shed workstation every weekday at 06:30 (Mr Lebel's normal shift start), and then this one anomalous 02:14 Saturday login. You cross-check with the resort's badge-log system (Splunk-indexed or queried by hand): t.lebel did not enter the building Friday evening or Saturday morning. Conclusion: the account is compromised, the credential is in someone else's hands, and the someone else has network access to the maintenance-shed workstation — possibly from a Wi-Fi range outside the building, possibly via an external VPN connection that ought not to exist.

You take three actions in the next ten minutes. (a) Disable the t.lebel Active Directory (AD) account; reset the credentials and revoke all active sessions. (b) Isolate the maintenance-shed workstation from the network (pull the cable; do not power it off — you want memory forensics to be possible if it comes to that). (c) Open an incident in your case-management tool with everything you have learned so far, timestamps included. Then you call your IR (Incident Response) retainer firm, because at 02:14 on a snow-grooming control system you are no longer dealing with a curiosity — you are dealing with the early phase of an Operational Technology (OT) intrusion, and that is a category of incident a generalist should not handle alone.

The lesson the source material highlights, and that I will re-state for the exam: the answer was already in the logs. Splunk found it in twelve seconds. The catch is that the logs had to be centrally indexed before the incident. If t.lebel's host had only been logging locally, the attacker would have wiped the Security log on the way out and you would have nothing.

▾ Case Study 10 — The Toronto bookstore USB drive at 23:48

(Anchored in Days 05–06 — Installation and Configuration of Monitoring Tools, Tool 10)

The situation. You are the part-time IT consultant for a small Toronto independent bookstore. The owner suspects someone is plugging a USB drive into the Point-Of-Sale (POS) Windows machine after hours. The store closes at 22:00 and reopens at 09:00. You install the Wazuh agent on the POS machine on Tuesday afternoon. On Thursday at 23:48 the dashboard lights up. Walk through what you see, how you respond, and how you would defend the response in court if it comes to that.

Model answer. Wazuh's relevance here is precisely what the source material highlights: HIDS (Host-based Intrusion Detection System) agents see endpoint behaviour that no network sensor will ever see. You configured the agent with the Sysmon integration enabled and a rule that fires on Windows Event ID 6416 ("a new external device was recognised by the system"). At 23:48 the rule fires and the dashboard shows: a USB drive labelled KINGSTON-32 was mounted at drive letter E: on the POS PC, and E:\setup.exe was launched approximately three seconds later by the SYSTEM account.

Three things happen in your next half-hour. (a) You remote into the POS over your management VPN — the bookstore has out-of-band management precisely because the source-material's twelve-smell checklist warned about not having it — and you isolate the machine: disable the network adapter, leave the host powered on (preserving volatile memory). (b) You upload a copy of \\POS\C$\Users\Public\setup.exe to VirusTotal and observe whatever detection ratio comes back. (c) You phone the owner to inform them, and — only with the owner's verbal consent followed by an email confirmation — you hand the actual USB stick (when it is physically retrieved on Friday morning) to the Royal Canadian Mounted Police (RCMP) cybercrime team. Chain of custody matters here: you photograph the USB stick, you sign and date a custody-transfer form, and you keep a copy of every artefact on your side.

If this goes to court (it probably won't — most cases close at the disciplinary stage — but you assume it will), the Wazuh-generated alert is your timeline anchor; the dashboard screenshot is your visual; the VirusTotal page is your indication of malicious intent; the chain-of-custody form is your evidentiary trail; and the fact that you remoted in over an out-of-band management path (not the same network the malware lived on) is your demonstration of professional care.

For the exam, the technical point to absorb is more compact: the alert is the easy part. The alert lit up because you wrote a rule for EventID=6416 in local_rules.xml — a five-line custom rule with a unique ID above 100000 (Wazuh's user-rule range). The hard part is everything that happened between the alert and the police hand-off. That is the SOC analyst's job.

▾ 3. Lab-safety rules

Several questions and case studies in this review describe attacks (ARP spoofing, NSE vulnerability scripts, USB-drop scenarios, ransomware reconstructions). They are described so that you can defend against them — not so that you can try them on networks you do not own.

The following rules are conditions of enrolment in this course:

  1. Run every offensive tool in an isolated lab. VirtualBox or VMware with a host-only adapter, or a dedicated, air-gapped subnet you control. Never on the college Wi-Fi. Never on a coffee-shop network. Never on a relative's home router.
  2. Targets are limited to (a) machines you personally own, (b) virtual machines provided in this course, or (c) public, written-consent ranges such as Hack The Box, TryHackMe and PortSwigger Web Security Academy. Nothing else.
  3. Snapshot, then break. Take a VM snapshot before every experiment. When the experiment ends — successful or not — revert. This habit will save you weeks of your life over a career.
  4. Document what you do. Date, time, target, command, observed result. If something ever goes wrong, your notes are the difference between a learning moment and a disciplinary file.
  5. Stop the moment something feels off. A box that you did not own and did not mean to touch, an exception you did not expect, an alert you cannot explain. Stop, disconnect, ask the instructor.
▾ 4. Legal compliance

Unauthorised access to a computer system, network or data — even briefly, even "just to look", even with no harm caused and no data taken — is a criminal offence in every jurisdiction we operate from. The four statutes below cover most of our cohort. If you are uncertain whether an action is lawful, the rule is simple: don't do it, and ask first.

Spain

Ley Orgánica 10/1995, de 23 de noviembre, del Código Penal — particularly Articles 197 bis, 264 and following, which criminalise unauthorised access to information systems, interception of data, and damage to computer systems.
🔗 Official Boletín Oficial del Estado text — Ley Orgánica 10/1995

European Union

Directive 2013/40/EU of the European Parliament and of the Council of 12 August 2013 on attacks against information systems, which sets minimum penalties across all E.U. member states for illegal access to information systems, illegal system interference, illegal data interference, and illegal interception.
🔗 Official EUR-Lex text — Directive 2013/40/EU

Canada

Criminal Code of Canada, Section 342.1 — Unauthorized use of computer. Any person who, fraudulently and without colour of right, obtains a computer service, intercepts a function of a computer system, or uses a computer system with intent to commit either of those offences, is liable to imprisonment for up to ten years.
🔗 Official Department of Justice text — Criminal Code s.342.1

United States of America

Computer Fraud and Abuse Act — 18 U.S.C. § 1030. Criminalises intentionally accessing a computer without authorisation or exceeding authorised access. Penalties range from fines and one-year sentences for first offences to twenty years in aggravated cases.
🔗 Official Cornell Law text — 18 U.S.C. § 1030
